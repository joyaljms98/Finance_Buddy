# Investment Basics: Risk, Return, Diversification, and Compounding

## The Risk‑Return Trade‑off

At the heart of investing is a simple truth: **higher potential returns come with higher risk.**

- **Low risk** – Your capital is relatively safe, but returns are modest, sometimes not even beating inflation.  
  Examples: Savings accounts, government bonds, fixed deposits (FDs), Public Provident Fund (PPF).
- **Medium risk** – Balanced funds, corporate bonds, debt mutual funds.
- **High risk** – Stocks, cryptocurrencies, small‑cap funds, startup investments. You can gain significant wealth, but you could also lose your principal.

## Diversification – Don’t Put All Eggs in One Basket

Diversification means spreading your investments across different **asset classes** and sectors. This reduces the impact of any single investment performing poorly.

**Example of a diversified portfolio:**
- 40% in large‑cap Indian stocks (e.g., through an index fund)
- 20% in international stocks (US/developed markets)
- 20% in debt (PPF, bonds, or debt mutual funds)
- 10% in gold (Sovereign Gold Bonds or ETFs)
- 10% in real estate (REITs or physical property)

By diversifying, you protect yourself against a crash in any one market.

## The Power of Compounding – The Eighth Wonder

Compound interest is when your **returns start earning returns**. The longer your money stays invested, the more dramatic the growth.

**Example:**  
You invest ₹1,00,000 at an annual return of 12%.
- After 10 years: ~₹3,10,000 (without adding more)
- After 20 years: ~₹9,65,000
- After 30 years: ~₹30,00,000

**Key takeaway:** Start early, even with small amounts. Time is your biggest ally.

## PPF – The “God Tier” Indian Investment

The Public Provident Fund (PPF) has **EEE status**:
- **E**xempt on investment (deduction under 80C in Old Regime)
- **E**xempt on interest earned
- **E**xempt on withdrawal at maturity

Even if you don’t get a deduction (e.g., under New Regime), the **~8% risk‑free, tax‑free** interest makes PPF an excellent low‑risk component of your portfolio. The lock‑in is 15 years, but partial withdrawals are allowed after 7 years.

## Final Rule for Beginners

**Before you buy your first stock:** Build an emergency fund of 6 months’ expenses and buy a health insurance policy (personal, not just company‑provided). Without these, a single emergency can wipe out years of investment gains.
